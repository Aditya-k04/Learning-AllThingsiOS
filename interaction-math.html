    Math for Interaction Design — Roadmap

    Start here — the plain idea

    Think of this whole course as learning one new pair of glasses. Right now, when you see a slick animation, you probably see moves — a card slides, a wave wobbles, cells tilt. Put these glasses on and you start seeing functions: a number going in (a finger, a clock, an item's index) and a number coming out (where, how big, how tilted). The magic word for this course is "model": turn the picture into a little math machine, then turn its knobs.

    The payoff is that you stop guessing. Instead of nudging "0.45" until it feels right, you describe the relationship once and everything downstream follows. The flagship example here is a wavy carousel where cells sit on a sine wave and tilt to match it — that one screen secretly uses a wave, a derivative (for the tilt), and an integral (for even spacing). Learn those few moves and you can place and aim anything on any path.

    This roadmap is the map. Each lesson is one tool in the kit; the order builds from "what's a function" up to the full carousel and then to how iOS actually computes these values frame by frame.

    The skill isn't "advanced math." It's a way of seeing: model the visual as a function, then use calculus / linear algebra to extract the geometric thing you need.

    This curriculum exists because of one caption on a wavy carousel:

    "Calculating the derivative of that wave lets you rotate the cells to sit perfectly on the curve. As you pinch, we stretch the amplitude of a sine wave that the cells follow."

    That sentence secretly contains trigonometry (the wave), a derivative (tangent → rotation), and nearby curvature (2nd derivative) and arc length (an integral). Become fluent in those moves and you can place and orient anything on anything. See the full decode in 08-worked-example-wavy-carousel.

    The one mental move

    position   y(x)      →  WHERE a thing is
tangent    y'(x)     →  which way it FACES   (rotation)
curvature  y''(x)    →  how hard it BENDS    (squash / scale)
arc length ∫√(1+y'²)dx →  distance ALONG the path (even spacing)

    Every interaction in this folder is an application of this. Internalize it and specs write themselves.

    01 — Modeling Visuals as Functions

    Start here — the plain idea

    A function is just a vending machine: you feed it something (a finger position, a clock, an item number) and it hands back something (a height, a tilt, an opacity). Modeling a visual "as a function" means refusing to think in vague moves — "it kind of slides up" — and instead naming the exact machine: in goes x, out comes y.

    Why bother? Because once it's a machine, calculus is just a set of buttons on the side of it. The derivative button tells you which way the curve is leaning right there — that's your rotation. The integral button adds up tiny steps along the path — that's your distance, for spacing things evenly. You don't compute these by hand; you just need to know what each button gives you geometrically.

    The picture to hold: imagine driving a car along a curvy road. Where you are is the position. The way your nose points is the tangent (the 1st derivative). How hard you're cranking the wheel is the curvature (the 2nd). And your odometer — total distance driven — is the integral. Same car, four different readouts. That's the entire lesson.

    The single habit that separates "I tweak values until it looks right" from "I can derive exactly what I need."

    Read this first — straight lines vs curves. Your school intuition — "acceleration = how fast speed changes" — is the 1D / straight-line picture. On a line there's only one thing to change: speed. There's no "turning" because there's nowhere for the direction to go.

    This whole topic is about curves in 2D (a thing sitting on a wavy path). Once a path bends, velocity is a vector — it has a speed and a direction — and those change independently:

    • change of speed → gas/brake (tangential)
    • change of direction → turning the wheel (the bend)

    So when these notes say a derivative gives "rotation/facing" or "how hard it bends," that's the direction half — the part that only exists once you leave a straight line. Feed a straight line in and the bend is just zero, which is exactly your linear intuition. Keep that split in mind and the rest clicks.

    The shift

    Stop asking "what animation API do I call?" Ask:

    What function describes this? And what does its derivative / normal / integral give me?

    Once a visual is a function, the whole toolkit of calculus and linear algebra becomes a set of design controls.

    The meta-move (memorize this)

    0th derivative   position    →  WHERE it is
1st derivative   tangent      →  which way it FACES  (rotation, velocity)
2nd derivative   curvature    →  how hard it BENDS   (acceleration, squash)
integral         accumulation →  arc length, area, "summed-up" quantities

    Each one hands you a new geometric handle on the same object. That progression is the "level of thinking" you're chasing.

    Memory aid: drive a car along the curve

    If the chain is hard to hold in your head, map it to the physics you already know — each step is just "the rate of change of the thing above it":

    Geometry term Physics analogy The car
    y(x) — position position where the car is on the map
    y'(x) — tangent velocity (1st deriv) the speedo + which way the nose points
    y''(x) — curvature acceleration (2nd deriv) how hard you turn the wheel
    ∫√(1+y'²)dx — arc length distance travelled the odometer
    • Position → velocity → acceleration are each one more derivative: how fast it moves, then how fast that changes.
    • Arc length is the odd one out — it's an integral, not another derivative. Derivatives break a quantity down; the integral builds it back up by accumulating tiny pieces over the trip. That's literally an odometer. (And it's not ∫y dx — that's the area under the curve. Arc length is ∫√(1+y'²)dx: each tiny step is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs dx and dy = y'·dx, so by Pythagoras its length is √(1+y'²)·dx. Sum them up. Full version: 03-derivative-as-tangent.)
    • Next rung if you ever need it: jerk (3rd derivative) = how abruptly the bending changes = the smoothness of the ride. Minimizing jerk is what makes premium easing feel buttery (see 06-interpolation-easing-and-springs).

    Why "acceleration = turning the wheel" (the honest version)

    Fair objection: isn't acceleration about changing speed? Yes — but velocity is a vector (speed and direction), and acceleration is the rate of change of that whole vector. So acceleration splits into two parts:

    • Tangential (along the motion) — changing speed: the gas/brake pedal.
    • Normal / centripetal (perpendicular) — changing direction: turning the wheel. (You feel this as the sideways push in a corner even at constant speed.)

    Curvature corresponds to the direction-changing part. The geometry chain y, y', y'' describes the shape of the path regardless of how fast you drive it, so it only "sees" the turning — not the gas. That's why curvature maps to the steering, and the speed-changing component simply doesn't appear in a static curve y(x) (it would only show up if you also tracked how fast a point travels along the path over time).

    Two more caveats: the physics chain differentiates w.r.t. time, the geometry chain w.r.t. x (same idea, different variable); and curvature isn't literally y'' but y''/(1+y'²)^{3/2}. Full treatment in 03-derivative-as-tangent.

    Worked micro-example

    Cells follow a wave y(x) = A·sin(ω·x + φ).

    • Where is cell i? → y(x_i)
    • Which way does it face so it sits flush on the curve? → tangent angle θ = atan2(y'(x_i), 1), and y'(x) = A·ω·cos(ω·x + φ)
    • Pinch stretches A → because y' is proportional to A, the tilt updates for free. One parameter, coherent result.

    Full derivation: 08-worked-example-wavy-carousel.

    Why this is the high-leverage skill

    • It makes effects composable: change one parameter, everything downstream follows correctly.
    • It makes them art-directable: you expose the parameters of the function (A, ω, φ) as sliders.
    • It removes guesswork: no more "0.45 felt right" — you derive the relationship.

    This isn't just about curves — it's a toolkit

    The wave is the flagship example, but the derivative chain is only one pillar. "Math for interaction" is a small set of tools you mix and match; the unifying mindset is always the same — model the thing as numbers you can compute, expose the parameters, and derive the relationships instead of guessing. The pillars:

    • Functions & the derivative chain (this lesson + 03-derivative-as-tangent) — place, orient, bend, and evenly space things along a path.
    • Trigonometry & the unit circle (02-trigonometry-and-the-unit-circle) — oscillation, rotation, waves, and radial/circular layout. sin/cos are everywhere.
    • Vectors & linear algebra (04-vectors-and-linear-algebra) — direction + magnitude; dot product = "how aligned?", cross = "which way does it turn?", rotation matrices, normals. The native language of 2D/3D space.
    • Parametric curves & splines (05-parametric-curves-and-splines) — describe any path (loops, Béziers, hand-drawn strokes), not just y = f(x).
    • Interpolation, remap & easing (06-interpolation-easing-and-springs) — lerp, inverseLerp, clamp, remap, smoothstep. This is the real workhorse of everyday UI: map a gesture/scroll position to an effect, smoothly. If you learn one thing here, learn remap + smoothstep.
    • Springs & physics (06-interpolation-easing-and-springs) — gesture-driven, interruptible, physical motion (the feel).
    • Oscillation, damping & noise (07-oscillations-noise-and-organic-motion) — motion that feels alive instead of mechanical.

    The carousel happens to use the derivative chain. A pull-to-refresh uses remap+spring. A breathing logo uses a sine. A draggable sheet uses vectors + rubber-banding. Different tools, same habit: turn the visual into a function of some input, then compute.

    Try it

    In Desmos: plot y = A sin(b x + c) with sliders for A, b, c. Then plot its derivative A b cos(b x + c) underneath. Drag A and watch both change together. That coupling is the whole idea.

    The foundation

    • function = a vending machine: put a number in, get a number out.
    • position y = where the thing is on the path.
    • tangent y' = the slope = which way it faces, so the tilt updates itself.
    • curvature y'' = how sharply it bends, for squash/scale on tight parts.
    • arc length ∫√(1+y'²)dx = the odometer — distance along the path, not area under it.
    • the meta-move = each derivative hands you a new geometric handle on the same object.
    • the toolkit = the wave is one pillar; vectors, curves, remap, springs, noise are the rest — same habit each time.

    01a — Choosing the Math (a Decision Recipe)

    Start here — the plain idea

    This lesson is a sorting hat. You describe your interaction in one plain sentence — "as X happens, Y changes" — and a couple of yes/no questions sort it into the right pile of math. You're not memorizing formulas; you're learning which drawer to open.

    The trick that makes this work: almost every interaction's input is one of a tiny set (time, a gesture, an index, a distance) and its output is one of a tiny set (position, scale, rotation, opacity, color). So really you're matching one small list to another, and the fork "does it map, cycle, or simulate?" decides the rest.

    The hardest part isn't the formulas — it's knowing which one to reach for. The secret: experienced people don't recall the right formula, they answer a few plain questions and the formula falls out. Here's that process, the way you'd build a formula from nothing.

    If you can't write the interaction as one sentence, you don't have an interaction yet — you have a vibe. Start with the sentence.

    Step 1 — Write it as one sentence: "as INPUT changes, OUTPUT changes"

    Force yourself to name both ends, with ranges:

    "As the finger drags down (input: 0 → 300 pt), the card shrinks (output: scale 1.0 → 0.5)."

    That sentence is the spec. In practice the input is almost always one of:

    • time (a clock ticking)
    • a gesture / scroll position (drag offset, contentOffset)
    • an index (item i of n)
    • a distance from some point or edge

    …and the output is almost always: position, scale, rotation, opacity, or color.

    Step 2 — One fork question: does it MAP, CYCLE, or SIMULATE?

    This single question picks the whole family of math:

    If the output… …reach for The plain need it answers
    goes A→B as input goes a→b lerp / remap / smoothstep (06-interpolation-easing-and-springs) "map this range onto that range"
    repeats, breathes, waves, goes in a circle sin / cos (02-trigonometry-and-the-unit-circle) "something that cycles forever, smoothly"
    has a position/direction in space vectors — dot = aligned?, cross = turn? (04-vectors-and-linear-algebra) "where / which-way in 2D/3D"
    sits or moves along a path/shape function + derivative chain (03-derivative-as-tangent) "where it is, which way it faces, how it bends"
    should settle, bounce, react to a flick a spring / physics (06-interpolation-easing-and-springs) "feel physical, not scripted"
    should feel alive / organic noise + summed sines (07-oscillations-noise-and-organic-motion) "random, but smooth"

    ~80% of real UI is the first row. When in doubt, the answer is almost always remap + smoothstep — "map a gesture/scroll position onto an effect."

    Step 3 — You don't memorize the building blocks; you can re-derive them

    The pieces aren't magic. Each was invented to answer a plain sentence, and you can rebuild the simple ones yourself:

    • "I want t=0 → start, t=1 → end, halfway → the middle." Write it: start + (end − start)·t. You just invented lerp.
    • "I want a value that cycles forever and stays smooth." A point going around a circle has a height that does exactly that → that's sin. Not arbitrary.
    • "I want to map one range onto another." That's lerp + "where is my input inside [a,b]?" (inverseLerp) + a clamp. Stack them → remap.

    So a beginner builds formulas by stating the need precisely and assembling a tiny vocabulary — not by recalling a textbook.

    The tiny vocabulary (90% of everything)

    lerp(a, b, t)        = a + (b − a)·t                 // blend a→b
inverseLerp(a,b,v)   = (v − a) / (b − a)             // "where is v in [a,b]?" → t
clamp(v, lo, hi)     = max(lo, min(hi, v))           // keep it in range
remap(v, a,b, c,d)   = lerp(c, d, clamp(inverseLerp(a,b,v),0,1))   // range → range
smoothstep(e0,e1,x)  = remap with eased ends         // gentle start & stop
sin(t) / cos(t)      = cycles / circular motion
spring(target)       = let physics settle it

    Master those seven and you can express most interactions. Full detail: 06-interpolation-easing-and-springs.

    Step 4 — Model it, don't derive it

    Don't try to calculate the perfect numbers in your head. Open Desmos (or a slider playground), wire the smallest building block, then drag the sliders and tune by feel. You discover the exact relationship by watching it, then read it off. Premium interactions are tuned, not computed cold. (This is the heart of 10-study-method.)

    Worked examples (sentence → formula)

    1. Card shrinks as you drag it away - Sentence: drag 0→300 makes scale 1.0→0.5. - Fork: it maps a range → remap. - Build: scale = remap(dragY, 0, 300, 1.0, 0.5). Want it gentle at the ends? wrap in smoothstep. - Release should spring back? That's a second interaction → fork says spring.

    2. A logo that gently breathes - Sentence: over time, scale oscillates 0.97 ↔ 1.03. - Fork: it cycles → sin. - Build: scale = 1.0 + 0.03 * sin(time * speed).

    3. Stories strip: items fade near the edges - Sentence: as an item's distance-from-center grows 0→1, opacity drops 1→0.3. - Fork: maps a range → remap + smoothstep. - Build: opacity = remap(distanceFromCenter, 0, 1, 1.0, 0.3); smoothstep for softness.

    4. Cells riding a wave (the carousel) - Sentence: cell at x sits on a wave and faces along it. - Fork: it follows a path → function + derivative. - Build: y = A·sin(ω·x), facing = atan2(y', 1). → 08-worked-example-wavy-carousel

    The whole recipe, in three questions

    What's my input? What's my output? Does it map, cycle, or simulate?

    Answer those and the math chooses itself — and the pieces are few enough to re-derive. "Which formula?" was never the real question; "what function describes this?" is, and that's the mindset from 01-modeling-visuals-as-functions.

    The foundation

    • the one sentence = "as INPUT changes, OUTPUT changes," with ranges — that is the spec.
    • the input = almost always time, a gesture, an index, or a distance.
    • the output = almost always position, scale, rotation, opacity, or color.
    • the fork = does it map, cycle, or simulate? — picks the whole family of math.
    • map = remap/smoothstep (and ~80% of UI lives here).
    • cycle = sin/cos; simulate = spring/physics; organic = noise.
    • re-derive, don't recall = state the need precisely and the formula falls out.

    01b — The Seven Building Blocks

    Start here — the plain idea

    Think of these seven as the LEGO bricks of motion. Almost nothing in UI is built from scratch — it's these few tiny functions snapped together. Most are about blending between two values; a couple are about cycling forever; one is a mini physics toy.

    The one to fall in love with is remap: "as THIS slides from A to B, make THAT slide from C to D." Two rulers taped together — point at the top ruler, read straight down to the bottom. Master that one and you've covered most of what you'll ever wire up.

    Almost every interaction is built from the same handful of tiny functions. Learn these seven and you can express most of what you'll ever need. Each one below is given once, with everything in one place: a plain-English idea, the formula, a mental picture, runnable SwiftUI code, and a number-trace so you can see it.

    If you remember only one thing from this whole topic, make it remap: "as THIS goes from A to B, make THAT go from C to D." That single idea covers most interactions.

    First, paste these helpers once — Swift doesn't ship lerp / remap / smoothstep:

    import SwiftUI

func lerp(_ a: Double, _ b: Double, _ t: Double) -> Double { a + (b - a) * t }
func inverseLerp(_ a: Double, _ b: Double, _ v: Double) -> Double { (v - a) / (b - a) }
func clamp(_ v: Double, _ lo: Double, _ hi: Double) -> Double { max(lo, min(hi, v)) }
func remap(_ v: Double, _ a: Double, _ b: Double, _ c: Double, _ d: Double) -> Double {
    lerp(c, d, clamp(inverseLerp(a, b, v), 0, 1))
}
func smoothstep(_ e0: Double, _ e1: Double, _ x: Double) -> Double {
    let t = clamp((x - e0) / (e1 - e0), 0, 1)
    return t * t * (3 - 2 * t)
}

    How to study each one: paste the snippet into an Xcode preview, then move the slider slowly while picturing the image — that's the moment the formula clicks.

    1. lerp — mix two values (the dimmer slider)

    Idea: give it a start, an end, and a percent → it returns the point that % of the way between them.

    lerp(a, b, t) = a + (b − a)·t

    Picture it: two pegs on a ruler — left = a, right = b. t is your finger sliding between them.

    Circle().position(x: lerp(40, 360, t), y: 40)   // t from a Slider 0...1

    Trace it: 40 + 320·t → t=0 → 40 (left) · t=0.5 → 200 (center) · t=1 → 360 (right).

    Use it for: moving, fading, or resizing between two known values. Works on numbers, points (lerp x and y separately), and colors (lerp each channel).

    Real example — a dot that smoothly chases your finger. Every frame, move it 15% of the way toward the finger:

    current = lerp(current, target, 0.15)   // run this each frame

    Far away → 15% of a big gap is a big step (fast). Close → 15% of a tiny gap is a tiny step (eases in). The same trick smooths a jumpy sensor value, or eases a number counter up to a new total.

    2. inverseLerp — un-mix: a value → a percent

    Idea: the reverse of lerp. You have a value between a and b; it tells you the percent that produced it.

    inverseLerp(a, b, v) = (v − a) / (b − a)

    Picture it: you're standing somewhere on that ruler and someone asks "what % of the way are you?"

    ProgressView(value: inverseLerp(0, 300, x))   // x from a Slider 0...300

    Trace it: x / 300 → x=0 → 0.0 · x=150 → 0.5 (bar half-full) · x=300 → 1.0.

    Use it for: turning a raw scroll/drag offset into a clean 0–1 progress you can feed elsewhere. ⚠️ Divide-by-zero if a == b; the result can fall outside 0–1 (that's what clamp fixes).

    Real example — the reading-progress bar at the top of an article. You know the scroll offset (say 0…2400 pt). Turn it into a 0–1 fill for the bar:

    let progress = inverseLerp(0, 2400, scrollY)   // 0.0 at top, 1.0 at bottom
ProgressView(value: progress)

    A raw position goes in, a clean percent comes out.

    3. clamp — stay in range (the wall)

    Idea: pin a value between a min and a max. Too low → snap to min; too high → snap to max.

    clamp(v, lo, hi) = max(lo, min(hi, v))

    Picture it: a ball on a leash inside a box — pull as far as you like, it stops dead at the wall.

    Circle().offset(x: clamp(drag.width, -80, 80))   // drag from a DragGesture

    Trace it: v=30 → 30 (inside) · v=200 → 80 (pinned right) · v=−500 → −80 (pinned left).

    Use it for: keeping opacity in 0–1, a drag inside bounds, or sanitizing an inverseLerp result.

    Real example — pinch-to-zoom that can't go too small or huge. Whatever wild number the gesture reports, keep it sane:

    let zoom = clamp(pinchScale, 1, 5)   // never below 1×, never above 5×

    The image zooms freely between 1× and 5× and just refuses to go past either limit.

    4. remap — translate one range to another ⭐ (two rulers taped together)

    Idea: the workhorse. "As the input goes from a→b, make the output go from c→d." It's literally the three above stacked: un-mix → clamp → mix.

    remap(v, a,b, c,d) = lerp(c, d, clamp(inverseLerp(a,b, v), 0, 1))

    Picture it: an input ruler (0…300, your scroll) above an output ruler (1.0…0.5, header scale). Point anywhere on the top; remap reads straight down to the matching spot below.

    Text("Header").scaleEffect(remap(scroll, 0, 300, 1.0, 0.5))   // scroll 0...300

    Trace it: scroll=0 → 1.0 (full) · scroll=150 → 0.75 · scroll=300 → 0.5 (half).

    Use it for: ~80% of interactions — "as this input moves through one range, drive that output through another." Swap the output to 1.0, 1.5 and the header grows instead; swap c and d to flip the direction.

    Real example — a "scroll to top" button that fades in as you scroll down. As scrollY goes 0→150, drive its opacity 0→1:

    button.opacity = remap(scrollY, 0, 150, 0, 1)   // hidden at top, fully visible after 150pt

    One input range, one output range, one line — and the clamp inside means it stays at 1.0 no matter how far you keep scrolling.

    5. smoothstep — remap with eased ends (the gentle car)

    Idea: like remapping into 0–1, but the result follows an S-curve — slow start, quick middle, slow finish. (easeInOut is basically this.)

    t = clamp((x − e0) / (e1 − e0), 0, 1)
return t·t·(3 − 2t)        // slope is 0 at both ends → no harsh snap

    Picture it: a car between two stop signs. A linear car jumps to full speed and slams the brakes; a smoothstep car pulls away gently, cruises, eases to a stop.

    Circle().position(x: 20 + w * t)                  // linear (even)
Circle().position(x: 20 + w * smoothstep(0,1,t))  // eased (slow–fast–slow)

    Trace it (watch it bend away from linear): t=0.1 → linear 0.10, smooth 0.028 · t=0.5 → both 0.50 (cross, fastest) · t=0.9 → linear 0.90, smooth 0.972.

    Use it for: edge fades, glow falloffs, softening any transition so it doesn't snap.

    Real example — dimming the background as a sheet slides open. As the sheet goes 0→1 open, fade a black overlay in gently so it doesn't pop:

    backdrop.opacity = smoothstep(0, 1, sheetProgress) * 0.5   // soft start, soft finish

    Plain remap would fade linearly (a faint snap at the edges); smoothstep eases in and out so the dim feels natural.

    6. sin / cos — repeat forever (the spinning wheel's shadow)

    Idea: a value that smoothly swings between −1 and +1 and repeats. A point on a circle at angle t is (cos t, sin t).

    value = A · sin(ω·t + φ)
        A = height (amplitude)   ω = speed (frequency)   φ = shift (phase)

    Picture it: a peg on a spinning wheel. Its shadow on the wall slides up and down forever — slowest at the extremes, fastest in the middle. That shadow is sin; the shadow on the floor is cos.

    TimelineView(.animation) { tl in
    let t = tl.date.timeIntervalSinceReferenceDate
    Circle().scaleEffect(1 + 0.2 * sin(t * 2))        // breathing: 0.8 ↔ 1.2
    Circle().offset(x: 80*cos(t*2), y: 80*sin(t*2))   // floor-shadow + wall-shadow = a circle
}

    Trace it: in 1 + 0.2·sin → sin=0 → 1.0 (rest) · sin=+1 → 1.2 (biggest) · sin=−1 → 0.8 (smallest). The ·2 spins faster (frequency); the 0.2 is how big the breath is (amplitude).

    Use it for: anything that repeats, breathes, waves, or sits on a circle. → 02-trigonometry-and-the-unit-circle

    Real example — a "recording" dot that pulses so users know it's live. Each frame, gently grow and shrink forever:

    dot.scaleEffect(1 + 0.1 * sin(time * 3))   // 0.9 ↔ 1.1, over and over

    time * 3 sets the pulse speed; 0.1 sets how big the pulse is. (Use cos + sin together to place items evenly around a circle, like a radial menu.)

    7. spring — move like a real object (the screen door)

    Idea: not a fixed path — a tiny simulation that pulls a value toward a target and lets it settle (with a little bounce if you want).

    a = −k·(x − target) − c·v        // pull toward target, minus drag
v += a·dt                        // k = stiffness, c = damping
x += v·dt                        // critical damping c = 2·√k → fastest, no overshoot

    Picture it: you let go of a screen door. It's pulled toward "closed," rushes over, maybe overshoots, wobbles, settles. You never drew the path; physics did.

    Circle().position(target)
    .animation(.spring(duration: 0.6, bounce: bounce), value: target)   // tap to set target

    Trace it: it measures the gap to the target and accelerates toward it; the closer it gets, the gentler the pull, so it slows naturally. bounce = 0 → eases in and stops clean · bounce = 0.5 → overshoots and wobbles first.

    Use it for: anything that should settle, bounce, or react to a flick/gesture. You only set target — and you can retarget mid-flight, which a fixed animation can't. → 06-interpolation-easing-and-springs

    Real example — a "like" heart that pops when tapped. Bump the scale and let a spring carry it there with a little overshoot:

    Image(systemName: "heart.fill")
    .scaleEffect(liked ? 1.3 : 1.0)
    .animation(.spring(bounce: 0.5), value: liked)   // pops, overshoots, settles

    You never animate the in-between frames — you flip liked and the spring makes it feel alive. Same idea snaps a bottom sheet open or a toggle into place.

    How they group in your head

    • lerp / inverseLerp / clamp are the three atoms; remap is them combined and is what you reach for daily.
    • smoothstep = remap with nice ends.
    • sin / cos = the cyclic family (things that repeat).
    • spring = the physical family (a simulation, not a formula).

    One-line recall: lerp = slide between two pegs · inverseLerp = ask "what %?" · clamp = hit a wall · remap = two rulers taped together · smoothstep = ease away & ease in · sin/cos = a spinning wheel's shadow · spring = a screen door settling.

    Try it

    You also have interactive versions in 06-interpolation-easing-and-springs: drag the easing demo to feel smoothstep vs a straight line, and the spring demo to tune k (stiffness) and c (damping) by hand.

    The foundation

    • lerp = slide between two pegs on a ruler; t is your finger.
    • inverseLerp = stand on the ruler and ask "what % am I?"
    • clamp = a ball on a leash that stops dead at the wall.
    • remap = two rulers taped together — read top input straight down to bottom output.
    • smoothstep = remap with a gentle car: ease away, cruise, ease to a stop.
    • sin/cos = a spinning wheel's shadow sliding back and forth forever.
    • spring = a screen door let go — pulled to its target, maybe overshoots, settles.

    02 — Trigonometry & the Unit Circle

    Start here — the plain idea

    Forget triangles for a second. Picture a dot walking around a circle at steady pace. Watch only its height over time and you get a smooth up-down wiggle — that wiggle is sin. Watch only its sideways position and you get the same wiggle, just shifted — that's cos. So a wave isn't some new thing; it's circular motion seen edge-on.

    That's why one idea unlocks three effects at once. Anything that breathes, pulses, or repeats forever is the height of that walking dot (sin). Anything that goes around — a radial menu, a loading spinner — is the dot's full (cos, sin) position. And rotating something is just nudging where the dot started on the circle.

    The three knobs on a wave are easy to feel: amplitude is how tall the wiggle is, frequency is how fast the dot walks (how many wiggles), and phase is where on the circle it began (slides the whole wave sideways). Pinch-to-stretch on the carousel? That's just turning the amplitude knob.

    Not "SOH-CAH-TOA." The one idea that unlocks waves, rotation, and oscillation at once.

    The core insight

    cos and sin are the x and y of a point travelling around the unit circle.

    point on circle at angle t  =  (cos t, sin t)

    So a wave is circular motion projected onto a line. Oscillation, rotation, and waves are the same phenomenon seen from different angles. Once you see this, half of "creative math" collapses into one picture.

    Anatomy of a wave

    y(x) = A · sin(ω·x + φ) + k
        │        │     │    └ vertical offset (baseline)
        │        │     └ phase   (horizontal shift / starting angle)
        │        └ angular frequency (how many radians per unit x → how tight)
        └ amplitude (height)
    • Amplitude A — how tall. (Pinch-to-stretch changes this.)
    • Frequency ω — how many wiggles. Period = 2π / ω.
    • Phase φ — slides the wave sideways; also = "where on the circle it starts."

    Things you can build immediately

    • Pulsing / breathing: scale = 1 + A·sin(ω·t) driven by time t.
    • Staggered wave across items: give item i a phase φ = i · Δ, so y_i = A·sin(ω·t + i·Δ) → a travelling wave through a list.
    • Circular layout: place item i at angle θ_i = i · 2π/n, position (R·cos θ_i, R·sin θ_i).
    // Travelling wave through a row of dots
for (i, dot) in dots.enumerated() {
    let phase = Double(i) * 0.4
    dot.offsetY = amplitude * sin(omega * time + phase)
}

    Bridge to rotation (important)

    A 2D rotation by angle θ is built entirely from sin/cos:

    [ cos θ  −sin θ ] [x]
[ sin θ   cos θ ] [y]

    So trig ↔ rotation ↔ linear algebra are one topic. Continue in 04-vectors-and-linear-algebra.

    Try it

    Desmos: plot (cos t, sin t) parametrically for t in [0, 2π] → you get a circle. Now plot (t, sin t) → you get the wave. Same sin, two viewpoints.

    The foundation

    • (cos t, sin t) = the x and y of a dot walking around the unit circle.
    • sin = that dot's height — a smooth wiggle, the same thing as a wave.
    • amplitude A = how tall the wiggle is (pinch-to-stretch changes this).
    • frequency ω = how fast the dot walks — how many wiggles; period = 2π/ω.
    • phase φ = where on the circle it started — slides the wave sideways.
    • stagger = give item i a phase i·Δ → a wave that travels down a list.
    • rotation = a 2×2 matrix built entirely from sin/cos — so trig is rotation.

    03 — Calculus: the Derivative as Tangent

    Start here — the plain idea

    The derivative sounds scary; geometrically it's just "the way the curve is leaning right here." Stand at any point on a hill and lay a ruler flat against the slope — the tilt of that ruler is the derivative. And a tilt is a direction, which is exactly what you need to rotate a cell so it sits flush on the curve. That's the whole carousel trick in one sentence.

    Three buttons, three pictures. The first derivative is that leaning ruler — which way it faces. The second derivative is how fast the lean is changing — how hard the curve bends (use it to squash things on tight corners). The integral is the odd one out: instead of breaking things down, it adds tiny pieces back up, like an odometer ticking off distance as you drive — that's how you measure length along a curve so you can space items evenly.

    So don't picture algebra. Picture a tangent line riding along a curve as you drag a point: its angle is your rotation, how quickly that angle swings is your curvature, and the running total of distance is your arc length.

    You don't need symbol-pushing. You need the geometric meaning of three operations.

    Derivative = slope = tangent direction

    The derivative y'(x) is the slope of the curve at x. Slope is a direction. That's why it gives you rotation.

    tangent vector at x  =  (1, y'(x))           // run 1, rise y'
tangent angle        =  atan2(y'(x), 1)      // rotate a cell by this to lie on the curve

    The six derivatives worth memorizing

    d/dx [ sin x ]   = cos x
d/dx [ cos x ]   = −sin x
d/dx [ x^n ]     = n·x^(n−1)
d/dx [ e^x ]     = e^x
d/dx [ c ]       = 0          (constant)
chain rule:  d/dx f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

    Chain rule is why d/dx [A·sin(ω x + φ)] = A·ω·cos(ω x + φ) — the inner ω pops out.

    Second derivative = curvature (how hard it bends)

    κ(x) = y''(x) / (1 + y'(x)²)^(3/2)

    Use it to squash or scale an element more on tight bends, or to drive "acceleration feel."

    Integral = accumulation → arc length

    To space items evenly along a curve (not evenly in x, which bunches them on steep parts):

    arc length from a to b  =  ∫_a^b sqrt(1 + y'(x)²) dx

    In code you usually do this numerically (walk the curve in tiny steps, sum segment lengths, then resample at equal distances).

    // Sample a curve, build a cumulative-length table, resample at even spacing
func arcLengthSamples(_ f: (Double)->Double, x0: Double, x1: Double, n: Int) -> [(x: Double, s: Double)] {
    var out: [(Double, Double)] = []
    var s = 0.0
    var prev = CGPoint(x: x0, y: f(x0))
    let dx = (x1 - x0) / Double(n)
    for i in 0...n {
        let x = x0 + Double(i) * dx
        let p = CGPoint(x: x, y: f(x))
        s += hypot(p.x - prev.x, p.y - prev.y)
        out.append((x, s)); prev = p
    }
    return out   // invert s→x to place items at equal arc-length
}

    The progression to keep in your head

    y      WHERE      position
y'     FACING     rotation / velocity   ← the carousel trick
y''    BENDING    curvature / accel
∫      ALONG      arc length / area

    Try it

    Desmos: plot a function and tangent(x0) at a draggable point. Drag it and watch the tangent line ride the curve — that line's angle is your rotation.

    The foundation

    • derivative y' = the slope = which way the curve leans here = a direction.
    • tangent → rotation = atan2(y', 1) rotates a cell to lie flush on the curve.
    • chain rule = peel an ω out: d/dx[A·sin(ωx+φ)] = A·ω·cos(ωx+φ).
    • second derivative → curvature = how hard it bends, to squash/scale on tight turns.
    • integral → arc length = add up tiny √(1+y'²) steps = an odometer along the curve.
    • even spacing = walk the curve, sum segment lengths, resample at equal distance.
    • the progression = y where · y' facing · y'' bending · ∫ along.

    04 — Vectors & Linear Algebra

    Start here — the plain idea

    A vector is just an arrow: it has a direction and a length, and that's it. The moment you stop juggling separate x and y and start thinking "arrow," your code shrinks and the geometry gets obvious. Position, velocity, an offset, the direction a cell faces — all arrows.

    Two little operations do most of the heavy lifting. The dot product answers "how much do these two arrows point the same way?" — big positive means aligned, zero means perpendicular, negative means opposing. Think "is the user dragging toward this thing?" The cross product (in 2D, a single signed number) answers "which way does the turn go — left or right?" — that's how you tell which side of a line you're on.

    One handy reflex: to get the perpendicular of an arrow (x, y), just swap and flip a sign → (−y, x). That's a 90° turn, and it's how you push a cell off to the side of a curve along its normal. And rotation? It's a tiny 2×2 grid of sin/cos numbers — the same trig from the last lesson, wearing a matrix costume.

    The native language of graphics. Two products do most of the work.

    A vector is a direction-with-length

    Position, velocity, normal, offset — all vectors. Learn to think in vectors instead of separate x/y and the code shrinks.

    import simd
let v = SIMD2<Float>(3, 4)
let len = length(v)          // 5
let dir = normalize(v)       // unit vector, length 1

    Dot product = "how aligned are these two directions?"

    a · b = ax·bx + ay·by = |a||b|·cos(angle between them)
    • > 0 → same general direction · 0 → perpendicular · < 0 → opposing.
    • Uses: lighting (N·L), "is the user dragging toward this?", projecting one vector onto another, finding angles.
    let alignment = dot(normalize(drag), normalize(target))  // 1 = straight at it

    Cross product (2D) = "how perpendicular / which way does it turn?"

    In 2D it's a single signed scalar:

    cross(a, b) = ax·by − ay·bx
    • Sign = turn direction (left/right, clockwise/ccw) → winding, "which side of the line."
    • Magnitude = area of the parallelogram → signed area, overlap tests.
    • Perpendicular (normal) of (x, y) is (−y, x) — rotating 90°. This is how you offset along a curve's normal.
    func normal(of tangent: SIMD2<Float>) -> SIMD2<Float> {
    normalize(SIMD2(-tangent.y, tangent.x))   // 90° turn
}

    Rotation is a matrix (and it's just sin/cos)

    R(θ) = [ cos θ  −sin θ ]      rotate point p:  p' = R(θ) · p
       [ sin θ   cos θ ]

    func rotate(_ p: SIMD2<Float>, by t: Float) -> SIMD2<Float> {
    SIMD2(cos(t)*p.x - sin(t)*p.y,
          sin(t)*p.x + cos(t)*p.y)
}

    Translation + rotation + scale compose by multiplying matrices — this is exactly what CGAffineTransform and CATransform3D are. See 15-transform-3d.

    Why this matters for the carousel

    The cell's rotation comes from a tangent vector; its perpendicular offset uses the normal (−y, x); placing it on a circle uses a rotation matrix. Same three tools, everywhere.

    Try it

    Take two draggable vectors in Desmos (or paper). Compute dot and cross by hand as you move them; predict the sign before you calculate. Build the reflex: dot = alignment, cross = turn.

    The foundation

    • vector = an arrow: a direction with a length. Think in arrows, not loose x/y.
    • dot product = "how aligned?" — + same way, 0 perpendicular, − opposing.
    • cross product (2D) = "which way does it turn?" — a signed number; also signed area.
    • normal (−y, x) = swap and flip = a 90° turn = how you offset sideways off a curve.
    • rotation matrix = a 2×2 grid of sin/cos — trig in matrix form.
    • compose by multiplying = translate · rotate · scale stacked = what transforms are.
    • carousel tie-in = tangent (arrow) for facing, normal for offset, matrix for circular layout.

    05 — Parametric Curves & Splines

    Start here — the plain idea

    So far a path was "height as you move right" (y = f(x)). But that can't draw a loop, a circle, or a vertical line — the path would have to be in two places at once. The fix is to stop describing the shape and start describing the trip: let one dial t run from 0 to 1, and at each moment say where the dot is, (x(t), y(t)). Now the dot can loop, cross itself, do anything — like an Etch A Sketch driven by time.

    The bonus is huge: the same derivative trick still works. The dot's velocity — how x and y are changing right now — points straight along the path. That's your tangent, on any curve, for free.

    Béziers are the everyday version: four points where two are endpoints and two are "handles" you tug to bend the curve, exactly like the pen tool in design apps. Catmull-Rom is the cousin that politely passes through every point you give it — perfect for "lay these cells smoothly along these spots." To place things evenly, you walk the curve in tiny steps, total up the length, and drop items at equal distances — the carousel recipe, now on any path.

    Generalize "the wave" to any path — circles, arcs, hand-drawn strokes, Béziers.

    Parametric form: describe motion, not just shape

    Instead of y = f(x), use a parameter t:

    P(t) = ( x(t), y(t) )          t usually in [0, 1]
P'(t) = ( x'(t), y'(t) )       velocity → TANGENT direction

    This handles loops and verticals that y = f(x) can't, and the tangent falls right out of the derivative — same trick as 03-derivative-as-tangent, now on any path.

    tangent angle at t  =  atan2(y'(t), x'(t))
normal              =  (−y'(t), x'(t))   normalized   // see 04

    Examples

    Circle:  P(t) = (R cos 2πt, R sin 2πt)
Wave:    P(t) = (t·W, A sin(ω t))
Spiral:  P(t) = (a·t·cos t, a·t·sin t)

    Bézier curves (the workhorse)

    A cubic Bézier is a blend of 4 control points P0..P3:

    B(t) = (1−t)³P0 + 3(1−t)²t P1 + 3(1−t)t² P2 + t³ P3
    • P0, P3 = endpoints; P1, P2 = "handles" that pull the curve.
    • Its derivative B'(t) (also a Bézier, one degree lower) gives the tangent → orientation along the path.
    • This is what UIBezierPath, CAShapeLayer paths, and the cubic timing curve all are. See 16-cashapelayer.

    Catmull-Rom (curve through points)

    Béziers don't pass through their middle controls; Catmull-Rom splines pass through every point — ideal for "lay cells along these positions smoothly." It's a few lines and converts to Béziers for drawing.

    Placing things along a curve

    1. Sample the curve → build a length table (03-derivative-as-tangent arc length).
    2. Resample at equal arc-length for even spacing.
    3. At each sample, take the tangent → rotate the element; take the normal → offset it.

    This is the wavy-carousel recipe generalized to any path: 08-worked-example-wavy-carousel.

    Try it

    UIBezierPath / Swift Charts / Desmos: drop 4 control points, drag the handles, and render the tangent at t = 0.5. Watch how the handles steer the orientation.

    The foundation

    • parametric form = describe the trip P(t)=(x(t), y(t)), not the shape — handles loops and verticals.
    • the parameter t = a 0→1 dial that drives the dot along the path.
    • tangent P'(t) = the dot's velocity = direction along the path, for free.
    • normal (−y', x') = the sideways direction, for offsetting off the path.
    • Bézier = 4 points: 2 endpoints, 2 handles you tug to bend it (the pen tool).
    • Catmull-Rom = a spline that passes through every point you give it.
    • placing on a curve = sample → length table → resample evenly → tangent rotates, normal offsets.

    06 — Interpolation, Easing & Springs

    Start here — the plain idea

    This is the workhorse lesson — the math behind most everyday UI. The core idea is blending: lerp slides between two values, and t (a 0→1 dial) is how far along you are. Stack a few of these and you get remap: "as the scroll goes from A to B, make the header scale go from C to D." That single sentence is most of interaction design.

    smoothstep is remap wearing nicer shoes — instead of a robotic constant pace, it eases away gently, speeds up, and eases to a stop, so transitions don't snap. That gentle S-curve is what "easeInOut" really is.

    Springs are a different animal. Instead of drawing a fixed path, you hand physics a target and let it settle there — like releasing a screen door. It rushes over, maybe overshoots and wobbles, then comes to rest. The beauty is you can change the target mid-flight (great for gestures), and "critically damped" just means "tuned to arrive fast with no bounce." Use blending when you know both endpoints; use a spring when something needs to feel physical and interruptible.

    The small toolkit you reach for in every falloff — UI or shader. (Deep companion: interpolation-and-easing.)

    The five functions to know cold

    lerp(a, b, t)      = a + (b − a)·t                 // blend a→b
inverseLerp(a,b,v) = (v − a) / (b − a)             // "where is v between a and b?" → t
clamp(v, lo, hi)   = max(lo, min(hi, v))
remap(v, a,b, c,d) = lerp(c, d, clamp(inverseLerp(a,b,v), 0, 1))   // map one range to another
smoothstep(e0,e1,x): t = clamp((x−e0)/(e1−e0),0,1); return t·t·(3 − 2t)

    remap + smoothstep are 80% of "make this value drive that effect smoothly." (The Stories-strip edge falloff was exactly smoothstep over a remapped distance.)

    func smoothstep(_ e0: Float, _ e1: Float, _ x: Float) -> Float {
    let t = max(0, min(1, (x - e0) / (e1 - e0)))
    return t * t * (3 - 2 * t)
}

    Easing = reshaping t before you lerp

    An easing curve is just a function [0,1] → [0,1] applied to progress. easeInOut ≈ smoothstep. Cubic timing curves are Béziers (05-parametric-curves-and-splines). See 14-animation-timing.

    Springs = physics instead of a fixed curve

    A spring is a second-order differential equation — which is why it connects back to calculus (03-derivative-as-tangent):

    a = −k·x − c·v          x = displacement from target
                        v = velocity, a = acceleration
                        k = stiffness, c = damping
v += a·dt ;  x += v·dt   // integrate each frame
    • Critical damping (c = 2·sqrt(k)) → fastest settle with no overshoot. (Used in the Perplexity dots' return.)
    • Under-damped → bouncy; over-damped → sluggish.
    • Springs are retargetable mid-flight (no fixed duration) → great for gestures. Full treatment: damped-harmonic-oscillator and the Wave library.
    // One critically-damped step
let damping = 2 * sqrt(stiffness)
let a = -stiffness * x - damping * v
v += a * dt
x += v * dt

    When to use which

    Need Use
    Map a position/scroll to an effect remap + smoothstep
    A→B over a fixed time, designed feel easing curve / Bézier timing
    Gesture-driven, interruptible, physical spring

    Try it

    Build a spring in a playground; expose k and c sliders. Find critical damping by feel, then verify c = 2√k.

    The foundation

    • lerp = blend a→b; t is how far along (0 to 1).
    • inverseLerp = the reverse — a value in, its t out.
    • clamp = keep a value inside its limits (hit a wall).
    • remap = "as input goes a→b, drive output c→d" — un-mix, clamp, mix. The workhorse.
    • smoothstep = remap with eased ends — slow start, slow finish, no snap.
    • easing = reshaping t before you lerp; easeInOut ≈ smoothstep.
    • spring = hand physics a target and let it settle; critically damped = fast, no overshoot; retargetable mid-flight.

    06a — Simulations Beyond the Spring

    Start here — the plain idea

    The spring taught you a powerful move: instead of drawing a motion curve, you give a tiny simulation a rule and let it generate the motion. The spring is just one member of a whole family that works this way — and once you have a few of them, you can make almost anything feel physical.

    They all share the exact same shape. You keep a little bit of state — usually a position, sometimes a velocity too — and every frame you update it from a small formula. Because the value reacts to a rule instead of following a script, the path emerges, and emergent paths are what read as "natural." The spring's a = −k·x − c·v was one such rule; here are the other rules worth owning.

    Think of this as your physics cabinet: reach for the one whose feel matches what you want (glide, bounce, snap, drift), tune one or two knobs by hand, and let the frame loop do the rest.

    Every formula below runs inside a per-frame loop (a TimelineView(.animation) in SwiftUI, or any 60–120 fps tick). dt is the seconds since the last frame — it keeps the motion the same real-world speed on any display. See 09-what-drives-the-value.

    1. Exponential smoothing — the "chase"

    No velocity, so it can never overshoot — it just eases toward a target and slows as it arrives.

    current += (target - current) * rate            // rate ≈ 0.1–0.2 per frame
// frame-rate-correct: current += (target - current) * (1 - exp(-dt / tau))

    Feel: calm ease-out, always settles gently. Use: smoothing a jittery sensor or cursor, easing a number counter, a soft follow. It's a spring with the bounce surgically removed — the same trick as the lerp "chase" from 01b-the-seven-building-blocks.

    2. Momentum / inertial decay — flick and glide

    Keep a velocity and bleed it off with friction each frame.

    position += velocity * dt
velocity *= pow(friction, dt * 60)              // friction ≈ 0.92; stop when |velocity| is tiny

    Feel: throw it and it coasts to a stop. Use: custom scroll views, swipe-to-dismiss, spinning a carousel or wheel. Pair it with #5 so it snaps to the nearest item as it slows.

    3. Rubber-banding — resistance past a limit

    Apple's own overscroll formula: the farther you drag past an edge, the harder it fights back.

    // x = how far past the edge you've pulled, d = the dimension (e.g. view height)
func rubberBand(_ x: CGFloat, _ d: CGFloat, c: CGFloat = 0.55) -> CGFloat {
    (1 - 1 / (x * c / d + 1)) * d
}

    Feel: the signature iOS "you can pull, but it resists, then springs back." Use: overscroll, dragging past bounds, pull-to-refresh. On release, hand the value to a spring (#lesson 06) to snap home.

    4. Gravity + bounce — the most literal physics

    Constant downward pull; flip and shrink the velocity on impact.

    velocity += gravity * dt                         // gravity ≈ 2000 pt/s²
position += velocity * dt
if position.y > floor {                           // hit the ground
    position.y = floor
    velocity.y = -velocity.y * restitution        // restitution ≈ 0.6 → each bounce smaller
}

    Feel: falling, bouncing, settling. Use: playful drops, confetti, a toast that plops in. restitution is your bounciness knob — 1 bounces forever, 0 is a dead thud.

    5. Attraction / magnetic snap — a spring toward the nearest anchor

    A spring whose target is recomputed each frame as the closest snap point.

    let target = nearestSnapPoint(to: position)       // grid line, page, detent
acceleration = -k * (position - target) - c * velocity   // then integrate (see lesson 06)

    Feel: magnetic — it drifts, gets grabbed, and clicks into place. Use: snap-to-grid, paging, bottom-sheet detents, ruler/wheel pickers.

    6. SmoothDamp — critically damped, but time-specified ⭐

    The most useful general-purpose one. It's a spring tuned so you say "settle in about N seconds" instead of guessing stiffness and damping — and it never overshoots.

    func smoothDamp(_ current: CGFloat, _ target: CGFloat,
                _ vel: inout CGFloat, smoothTime: CGFloat, dt: CGFloat) -> CGFloat {
    let omega = 2 / smoothTime
    let x = omega * dt
    let exp = 1 / (1 + x + 0.48*x*x + 0.235*x*x*x)
    let change = current - target
    let temp = (vel + omega * change) * dt
    vel = (vel - omega * temp) * exp
    return target + (change + temp) * exp
}

    Feel: the smoothness of a spring with the predictability of a duration. Use: camera/scroll follow, easing a value where you want "arrives in ~0.3s" but with zero bounce. This is the sweet spot between #1 and a full spring.

    7. Verlet / trailing follow — chains and tails

    Each segment chases the one ahead of it, so a lag ripples down the line.

    for i in 1..<nodes.count {
    nodes[i] += (nodes[i-1] - nodes[i]) * k        // each node lags the previous
}
// true Verlet for rope/cloth: x_new = x + (x - x_prev) * damping + accel * dt * dt

    Feel: ropey, comet-tail, snake-like. Use: cursor trails, connected-dot effects, a dangling handle, cloth.

    8. Noise wander — organic idle

    Not a simulation exactly, but the same spirit: drive a value from smooth noise instead of a fixed curve, so it's alive and never repeats.

    x = baseX + amplitude * noise(t * frequency)       // 1D drift
// 2D: two noise fields with different seeds for x and y

    Feel: gently breathing, floating, hand-held-camera. Use: idle-state motion, ambient background elements, making anything feel less mechanical. Full treatment in 07-oscillations-noise-and-organic-motion.

    How to pick one

    The through-line is why they all feel natural: each keeps state and updates it from a local rule, so the path emerges instead of being drawn — the same move as the spring. Bucket them by feel:

    You want… Reach for
    Ease to a target, no overshoot exponential smoothing (#1), SmoothDamp (#6)
    Something that was thrown inertial decay (#2), gravity (#4)
    Constraints & boundaries rubber-band (#3), magnetic snap (#5)
    Coupled / many-body verlet trailing (#7)
    Organic / ambient noise (#8)

    Two you'll reach for constantly: SmoothDamp (best default "follow a target") and inertial decay + snap (best default "flickable list").

    The foundation

    • the family move = keep state (position, maybe velocity) and update it each frame from a rule; the path emerges — same as the spring.
    • exponential smoothing = ease to a target by a fraction of the remaining gap; no velocity, no overshoot.
    • inertial decay = carry a velocity and multiply it by friction each frame → flick-and-glide.
    • rubber-band = resistance that grows the farther you pull past a limit (the iOS overscroll formula).
    • gravity + restitution = constant pull plus a velocity flip on impact → falling and bouncing.
    • magnetic snap = a spring whose target is the nearest anchor, recomputed each frame.
    • SmoothDamp = a critically damped spring you tune by settle time — smooth like a spring, predictable like a duration.
    • verlet trailing = each node chases the one ahead → ropes, tails, cloth.
    • dt = seconds per frame; multiply rates by it so motion runs the same speed on 60 and 120 Hz.

    07 — Oscillations, Noise & Organic Motion

    Start here — the plain idea

    A plain sin wave wiggles forever at the same size — fine for a steady pulse, but real things lose energy. Multiply the wave by a fading curtain (e^(−λt), a shrinking envelope) and the wiggle dies down over time — that's a bounce settling, the natural cousin of a spring.

    Two more tricks make motion feel alive instead of looping. First: stack a few sine waves at speeds that don't line up neatly, and the result never visibly repeats — it just drifts, gently, forever. (Pick "ugly" numbers like 0.6 and 0.43 so they never sync.) Second: noise. Regular random() jitters like static; Perlin/simplex noise is smooth random — ask it about two nearby moments and it gives you two nearby answers. That smoothness is exactly what reads as organic — smoke, a flag, a hand's slight tremor.

    The pro recipe layers these: a resting position, plus slow summed-sine breathing, plus a touch of noise to wander, plus a spring so it still reacts to your touch. Boring parts, alive result.

    How to make motion feel alive instead of mechanical.

    Damped oscillation = bounce that dies out

    y(t) = A · e^(−λt) · sin(ω t + φ)
        │    │          └ the wiggle
        │    └ exponential decay envelope (λ = decay rate)
        └ initial amplitude

    The e^(−λt) envelope is the secret to natural settling — it's the analytic cousin of the spring in 06-interpolation-easing-and-springs. See damped-harmonic-oscillator.

    Summed sines (Fourier intuition)

    Any periodic motion is a sum of sine waves.

    Layer a few sines at different frequencies/amplitudes and you get rich, non-trivial motion from trivial parts:

    y(t) = Σ  A_i · sin(ω_i · t + φ_i)

    Use incommensurate frequencies (not simple multiples) so it never visibly repeats — that's the "gently alive, never looping" feel (the Perplexity dots' idle drift used exactly this).

    let idle = a1*sin(0.6*t + p1) + a2*sin(0.43*t + p2)   // 0.6, 0.43 don't sync

    Noise = controlled randomness with smoothness

    Plain random() is jittery. Perlin / simplex noise is smooth random — neighboring inputs give nearby outputs. That smoothness is what reads as organic (smoke, flowing fields, hand-tremor).

    • Sample noise over time for wandering motion: offset = noise(seed, t·speed).
    • Sample over space for fields/turbulence: n = noise(x·scale, y·scale).
    • Domain warping (feed noise into the input of more noise) gives liquid, billowing motion. See 15-noise-and-patterns.

    Recipe: "gently alive" element

    position = rest
         + summedSines(t)          // slow breathing      (this note)
         + noise(seed, t)·amp      // organic wander       (this note)
         + springToTarget(...)     // responds to input    (06)

    That layered structure is how the best ambient interactions are built.

    Try it

    In a shader or playground, animate a dot with one sine, then add a second at an unrelated frequency, then add a touch of noise. Feel each layer's contribution.

    The foundation

    • damped oscillation = a wiggle times a fading curtain e^(−λt) → a bounce that dies out.
    • decay rate λ = how fast the envelope shrinks (how quickly it settles).
    • summed sines = any periodic motion is a stack of sine waves layered together.
    • incommensurate frequencies = "ugly" non-multiple speeds (0.6, 0.43) so it never visibly loops.
    • noise = smooth random — nearby inputs give nearby outputs (vs jittery random()).
    • domain warping = feed noise into noise → liquid, billowing motion.
    • "gently alive" recipe = rest + summed sines + noise wander + spring to target.

    08 — Worked Example: The Wavy Carousel

    Start here — the plain idea

    This is the victory lap — the one screen that ties the whole course together. Picture a row of cards riding a gentle sine wave, each tilted so it lies flush on the curve like beads on a wire, and when you pinch, the wave grows taller and the tilts adjust themselves. Every tool you've learned shows up here, working as a team.

    The chain is short and satisfying. Put each cell on the wave to get its height (sin). Take the slope at that spot to get its tilt (the derivative). And here's the magic: the slope is proportional to the wave's height, so when you pinch to stretch the wave, the tilts update for free — one knob, coherent result. The fancier touches (spacing cells by true distance along the wave, squashing them slightly on tight bends) are just arc length and curvature making a cameo.

    So as you read, watch for the cast: a wave, a derivative, a remap on the pinch, and an integral for spacing. Nothing new — just the kit, assembled.

    The full decode of: "the derivative of that wave lets you rotate the cells to sit on the curve; as you pinch, we stretch the amplitude." Everything in this folder, applied once.

    Step 1 — Model the curve as a function

    Cells ride a sine wave along the horizontal axis x:

    y(x) = A · sin(ω·x + φ)

    A = amplitude (the pinch target), ω = frequency, φ = phase. → 02-trigonometry-and-the-unit-circle

    Step 2 — Place each cell

    Cell i sits at some x_i (its scroll position). Its vertical offset:

    y_i = A · sin(ω·x_i + φ)

    Step 3 — Derivative → tangent → rotation

    The slope at x_i (chain rule, 03-derivative-as-tangent):

    y'(x) = A · ω · cos(ω·x + φ)

θ_i = atan2( y'(x_i), 1 )      // tangent angle
cell.rotation = θ_i            // now it lies flush on the curve

    Step 4 — Pinch stretches amplitude (and rotation follows for free)

    Map the pinch gesture's scale onto A:

    A = remap(pinchScale, 1.0, 3.0,  Amin, Amax)    // see 06

    Because y' is proportional to A, increasing amplitude automatically increases the tilt. One parameter drives shape and orientation coherently — that coupling is the craft.

    Step 5 (level-up) — even spacing via arc length

    Equal x spacing bunches cells on the steep parts. Space them by arc length instead (03-derivative-as-tangent):

    s(x) = ∫ sqrt(1 + y'(x)²) dx     // distance along the wave

    Sample the curve, build a length table, resample at equal s. Now cells are visually even no matter how tall the wave gets.

    Step 6 (extra polish) — curvature

    Scale/foreshorten cells slightly by curvature κ = y'' / (1 + y'²)^{3/2} so they "hug" tight bends. → 03-derivative-as-tangent

    The whole thing in pseudo-code

    on gesture or frame:
    A = remap(pinchScale, 1, 3, Amin, Amax)
    for each cell i at x_i:
        y      = A * sin(ω*x_i + φ)
        slope  = A * ω * cos(ω*x_i + φ)
        θ      = atan2(slope, 1)
        cell.position = (x_i, baseline + y)
        cell.rotation = θ
        # optional: cell.scale = 1 - clamp(curvature)*k

    Build it yourself (Week 4–5)

    Do it in SwiftUI first (rotationEffect + offset per cell, driven by a MagnifyGesture's scale), then understand it as a pure function of (x_i, A). This is the function-driven mindset from 09-what-drives-the-value.

    The foundation

    • model the curve = y(x) = A·sin(ωx+φ) — cells ride a sine wave.
    • place a cell = plug in its x → y_i = A·sin(ωx_i+φ).
    • tilt it = slope y' = A·ω·cos(ωx+φ), rotation = atan2(y', 1) → lies flush.
    • pinch → amplitude = A = remap(pinchScale, …); tilt follows because y' ∝ A.
    • the coupling = one parameter drives shape and orientation coherently — the craft.
    • even spacing = arc length ∫√(1+y'²)dx, resample at equal distance.
    • polish = curvature κ squashes cells slightly on tight bends.

    09 — What Drives the Value (the iOS Bridge)

    Start here — the plain idea

    The rest of the course told you what number you want — the height, the tilt, the opacity. This lesson asks a different question: who keeps recomputing that number, and when? Same effect can be wired three very different ways, and picking the right one is what makes an interaction feel clean instead of janky.

    Three drivers, in plain terms. First, input-driven: the value is just a function of where your finger or scroll is right now — no animation at all, it's literally f(scrollOffset). The wavy carousel and Stories edge-scaling live here, which is why they feel glued to your finger. Second, a tween: you name a start and end and let the system smoothly blend between them — a button press, a sheet opening. Third, per-frame: you recompute every single frame in a loop, needed for ongoing simulations like particles or physics fields.

    The discipline is to name three things before you code: what function gives the value, which driver owns it, and which layer (Core Animation / Metal) actually draws it. Get those three and the implementation is nearly mechanical.

    The math tells you what the value should be. This note tells you how iOS should compute it over time. Same three categories, every interaction.

    The three drivers

    Driver The value is… iOS tools Example
    Input-driven (a pure function) f(scrollOffset) or f(gesture) — no animation visualEffect, scrollTransition, UIScrollView.contentOffset, gesture translation Stories edge-scale; the wavy carousel
    Interpolated (a tween) a start→end the system blends SwiftUI .animation, UIViewPropertyAnimator, CA implicit/explicit a button press, a sheet
    Per-frame (imperative) recomputed each frame CADisplayLink, Metal compute, manual loop Perplexity dots; physics fields

    The carousel and the Stories strip are category 1 — they are functions of gesture/scroll, not animations. That's why "no per-frame effect, apply to the main composition" was the right instinct.

    How to choose

    1. Is the value a direct function of a gesture/scroll position? → pure function (cleanest; prefer this).
    2. Is it "go from A to B with a nice feel"? → tween / spring.
    3. Does it need continuous simulation (particles, fields, frame-precise timing)? → per-frame.

    Why understanding Core Animation matters here

    SwiftUI and UIKit are skins over Core Animation. The math you compute becomes layer transforms that the render server interpolates. Knowing the substrate explains why category-2 tweens behave as they do (model vs presentation layer) and when to drop to category 3.

    • Substrate: Core Animation Roadmap → 01-how-rendering-works, 15-transform-3d
    • Springs at the CA level: 11-caspringanimation
    • GPU per-frame: 24-metal-and-calayer, Shaders Roadmap

    The discipline

    When you imagine an interaction, name three things before coding:

    1. What function describes the value? (this folder)
    2. Which driver owns it? (this note)
    3. Which layer renders it? (Core Animation / Metal)

    Get those three and the implementation is almost mechanical.

    The foundation

    • input-driven = value is f(gesture/scroll), no animation — feels glued to your finger (the carousel).
    • interpolated (tween) = name start→end, system blends — button press, sheet.
    • per-frame = recompute every frame in a loop — particles, physics, frame-precise timing.
    • how to choose = direct function of input? prefer that; "A→B with feel"? tween; ongoing sim? per-frame.
    • the substrate = SwiftUI/UIKit are skins over Core Animation; your math becomes layer transforms.
    • name three things = what function · which driver · which layer — then it's mechanical.

    10 — Study Method

    Start here — the plain idea

    Math like this doesn't stick from reading — it sticks from fiddling. The single most important habit on this page: before you write a line of code, build the interaction as a function in Desmos and drag the sliders. Watching the tangent swing as you move a point teaches your gut something no paragraph can.

    The loop is simple: spot an interaction you love, guess its function and driver, rebuild it in Desmos with sliders, derive whatever it needs (tangent, normal, curvature), then build it for real and compare. Ten honest reps of that beat any course. Treat your shader playground as a lab — it already speaks smoothstep, vectors, and oscillation, so new concepts have somewhere to land immediately. The anti-pattern to avoid: jumping straight to code and tuning magic numbers, instead of modeling first and deriving the relationship.

    The method matters more than the reading list. Four habits.

    1. Desmos first, code second

    Before writing any code, model the interaction as a function in Desmos with sliders. Take its derivative there; watch the tangent move. The interactivity builds intuition that reading can't. This is the most important habit on the page.

    2. Drill the meta-move

    position → derivative → geometric quantity. Pick a curve (wave, circle, spiral, Bézier) and extract its tangent (orientation), normal (perpendicular), and curvature (bend). Do it for five different curves until it's automatic. → 01-modeling-visuals-as-functions, 03-derivative-as-tangent

    3. Reconstruct, math-first

    You already reverse-engineer interactions — keep doing it, but write the function and derive what you need before building. Ask: what curve are the cells on? what's its derivative? what does the gesture change? Write that spec, then code. (This also trains the spec/pseudo-code muscle — turn "scales as it enters" into scale = lerp(0.5, 1, smoothstep(t)) with named constants and stated edge cases.)

    4. Use shaders as the lab

    Your Metal work is applied math — smoothstep, vectors, oscillation are already in there. When you learn a concept, immediately deploy it in a shader. Theory + an existing playground = a fast feedback loop. → 05-math-for-shaders

    A good single rep

    1. See an interaction you like.
    2. Guess the function + driver (09-what-drives-the-value).
    3. Reproduce it in Desmos with sliders.
    4. Derive the tangent/normal/whatever it needs.
    5. Build it (SwiftUI or a shader).
    6. Compare to the original; note what you missed.

    Ten of these reps will move you further than any course.

    Anti-patterns

    • Memorizing formulas without the geometric picture → you'll forget them. Learn the meaning (slope, alignment, turn).
    • Jumping to code before modeling → you end up tuning magic numbers instead of deriving relationships.
    • Reading passively → always have Desmos or a playground open.

    The foundation

    • Desmos first, code second = model with sliders before coding — the top habit.
    • drill the meta-move = take tangent, normal, curvature on five different curves until automatic.
    • reconstruct math-first = write the function and derive what you need before building.
    • shaders as the lab = deploy each new concept in a playground for instant feedback.
    • one good rep = see · guess · Desmos · derive · build · compare.
    • anti-pattern = jumping to code and tuning magic numbers instead of modeling first.
